Pay,
Publish, Profit!
How fraudulent research papers poison the sacred tree of knowledge.
Data narrative by Chandana
"Science runs on brainpower and funding but also trust,"
— Jennifer Byrne,
Professor of Molecular Oncology
at the University of Sydney
Trust that what you’re studying is important.
Trust that your work and integrity will be rewarded by solid results that will steer further studies.
After all, we stand on the shoulders of giants –the findings of those that came before inform our research today.
So we trust that what we know is, in fact, true and credible.
And we push our boundaries of knowledge by building on what we believe to be sturdy ground.
That, after all, the steps we ascended
were just made of
paper?
That’s exactly what Australian pediatric cancer researcher Jennifer Byrne felt when she discovered that the little-known subject of her study, a certain gene that might have implications for cancer treatment, have been the subject of multiple different papers by various authors-

-and they were all fake!
But what happens when the
ground beneath our feet gives out?
Byrne discovered a single paper from China first, that reminded her of her own findings in 1998. She realised the images, the methods and the results were almost identical to her own findings.

And then she found another…and then another! She realised it was actually 5 papers that were identical-and none were without flaws or mismatched data.

Byrne says the results obtained were highly unlikely, even impossible to get.

Then she found 43 more.
It is believed that such papers likely originate from an academic paper mill: Shady companies that make a business out of fake academic papers.
These companies churn out poorly written papers written by a ghostwriter, (who is now likely being replaced by generative AI tools like ChatGPT)
They then publish the paper to an unheard-of journal via their own dubious publishing house,

or bribe editors on boards of more reputable journals to accept them.
The paper is then reported to the journal, and eventually retracted, that is, unpublished by the journal.
Desperate researchers trying to get published buy these slots, with no idea about where the paper might get published, what it might contain, or who the co-authors are.
These papers fly under the radar until someone gets suspicious, or discuss it on sites like PubPeer.
Then they advertise authorship slots online for hefty prices.
The menace of fraudulent papers was severe enough for Adam Marcus and Ivan Oransky to start Retraction Watch, an online blog-turned-database that tracks the details of retractions world-wide.

And the numbers are not looking good.
While retractions on their own don’t mean paper mill activity, many retractions have been flagged as paper mill content and other such fraudulent or otherwise dubious origins.

As per the Retraction Watch database, a large and growing chunk of retracted papers are from paper mills.
47% of retractions are from
paper mills,
have issues with data,
or have randomly generated content
It turns out papermilling is a rather profitable business. While China is the market leader by a huge margin, India is the next highest on the list.
But these numbers are nothing compared to the loss of human life due to researchers wasting time, money and energy on studying fake results and trying to replicate them. Especially in cancer research and the broader biomedical sciences.
Hyderabad-based publisher OMICS reportedly had a gross revenue of at least $50 million from 2011-2017.
The estimated combined revenue of a handful of paper mills from India is about $30 million.
The estimated annual revenue of the global academic paper mill industry is about $3 billion.
In an estimation by Byrne in 2022, we can see a measurement from a different perspective.
1/4th of the 712 problematic papers studied will potentially create misinformation
Researchers, especially early in their careers, are desperate to get published despite having neither the time nor the resources to conduct proper research.

Countries like China, India, Russia and Iran have quotas for publications that are required for further career opportunities, such as tenure at an institute, or offer cash incentives on a per-paper basis, which also encourages other types of misconduct, such as salami-slicing data for several papers.
This kind of policy is aptly named
and paper mills profit off of this desperation, uncaring towards the cost to science.
‘Publish or Perish’
Institutes, on the other hand, set such quotas in a race to climb rankings, such as the NIRF in India, which base their rankings on the number of papers published and their impact factors rather than the quality of research.
The most obvious harmful consequence of all this, of course, is the decline of trust in scientists and publishing.

While the paper that fraudulently claimed that the MMR vaccine causes autism has since been retracted, it has spawned a movement against vaccines that has brought back many preventable diseases from the dead.

Critics say we’re doing too little, too late. But there’s still hope.
